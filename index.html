Reference

Decision Rules

10 Rules From Past Events

  • One decision-maker per category. Not committee. One person decides, informs the rest.
  • Design freeze is sacred. No new requests after [date]. Communicate to ALL including founders.
  • Payment authority in event timezone. Card holder awake OR team has pre-authorized limit.
  • Artwork deadline = printer minus 48 hours. Not same-day. Non-negotiable.
  • Editor brief = deliverables + references + turnaround + "what good looks like."
  • Backup plan documented before it's needed. If primary falls through, Plan B is already named.
  • Daily status update from each lead during final week. One message: where I am, what's blocking, what I need.
  • Conflicting instructions = flag immediately, don't pick one. One message to both people: "[Person A] said X, [Person B] said Y — which one?" Stop. Wait. Don't execute either until resolved. US event: Simon said "draft" on Shopify, Rachana said "unlisted." One instruction was followed without flagging the conflict. Caught days later. Escalate, don't guess.
  • Schedule changes = communicate to all affected parties within 24 hours. If dates, venues, or timelines shift, everyone whose work depends on it needs to know immediately. Changes within 4 weeks of the event need Momo's sign-off. Changes within 2 weeks need Momo + Rachana together. A shifted date affects shipping, vendors, travel, content plans, and staffing. The earlier the team aligns, the less rework. Agree on a realistic timeline together rather than adjusting silently.
  • Extended hours need a heads-up, not a surprise. If event prep will require work beyond regular hours, flag it at least 1 week before: how many extra hours, which days, and what specifically needs the extra time. Momo or Crystal confirms so the team can plan around it. Events naturally have crunch periods. Naming it early lets the team prepare, swap tasks, and protect rest days — instead of discovering the workload at the last minute.

Escalation & Communication

  • No vendor reply in 24hrs: Call. Not another message.
  • Design file 48hrs late: Escalate to Crystal + Momo immediately.
  • Founder silent 4hrs during event week: Decide yourself (within authority), inform after.
  • Someone AWOL: Reassign tasks immediately. Debrief later.
  • "Under [X amount], just get it" rule: Define threshold at kickoff.
  • Pin decisions in WA. Stops re-debates.
  • Voice notes for context, text for decisions. Decisions must be searchable.

Direction, Ownership & Authority

Who Decides What

  • Creative / content direction → Crystal is final say. Brand aesthetic, edit style, what gets posted, design quality. If Crystal says X and Momo says Y on a creative decision, Crystal wins. Crystal Jul 9: "I'm very strict on content because I know we can all do a really good job now."
  • Operations / logistics / budget → Momo is final say. Vendor selection, spending, timelines, staffing, venue decisions. Crystal doesn't need to weigh in on storage units.
  • Guest list / venue / on-ground logistics / warehouse & shipping ops → Rachana is final say. Capacity, ticketing, parking, volunteers, delivery coordination, Portless, GPS, SKU process. When Simon and Rachana give conflicting ops instructions, Rachana's version is correct.
  • Talent / creator sourcing → Britt is final say. Who to bring, negotiation, local vendor relationships.
  • Rahul overrides all — but Crystal must always know. When Rahul gives direction in the event WA group, that IS the decision (everyone saw it). When Rahul gives direction in DMs or separate calls, Dione's first move: message Crystal "Rahul just said X — does this change what we're doing?" Rahul drops in unpredictably with strategic calls (London: "limited spots, first come first served"). His word stands — but Crystal must know so she doesn't work against it.
  • When Crystal and Momo conflict → Dione flags, doesn't choose. One message to both: "Crystal said X, Momo said Y — which one?" Stop trying to satisfy both silently. When two leads give different directions, flag it immediately instead of trying to satisfy both.

Lead vs Support (Ownership Rules)

  • LEAD = you drive it, you're accountable for the outcome. You don't wait for someone to tell you to move. You set the deadline, you chase, you deliver.
  • SUPPORT = you help when asked, you remind, you follow up. You don't own the outcome. You unblock the lead.
  • Every shared task must name one LEAD. No "/" without clarity. "Crystal / Dione" means nothing if neither knows who drives. Dione's feedback: "It's very important to know who leads. 'Set artwork deadline' is Crystal's task — I support by reminding and following up."

Dione's Authority

  • After Crystal's first approval on any new format, Dione runs with it. First poster approved → Dione can produce the rest in the same style without checking each one. First social post approved → Dione schedules the rest. Crystal Jul 9: "The first few times, me, you, and Momo sit down and clarify. Then once it's good, you're basically on your own."
  • Dione can brief and direct external teams (Forge, editors, printers) directly. No need to go through Crystal or Momo for every interaction. Crystal Jul 9: "I want to dedicate you to be the point of contact. For example, meeting with Forge."
  • Dione is the quality gate before founders. If content isn't good enough, Dione sends it back before Crystal/Momo/Rahul ever see it. Crystal Jul 9: "If you can't catch it first before getting to the founder, then we did our job."
  • Dione can nudge and follow up with anyone — vendors, editors, agency, Crystal, Momo — without asking permission. Following up is not overstepping. Silence is the problem, not nudging.
  • Dione flags blockers same-day. If she's waiting on an approval, a file, or a response for more than 4 hours during event week, she escalates. Not "I'll wait until the next sync." Waiting for responses costs days. If blocked 4+ hours during event week, escalate — don't wait for the next sync.
